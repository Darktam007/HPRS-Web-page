The Founder

I Sold Nescafé on the Street.
I Was Already Reading Books That Would Change My Life.

Let me tell you where I started.

Not a classroom. Not a comfortable home office. Not a place that looks anything like where I am today.

I started on the street. Literally.

I was that young man walking up to strangers, selling Nescafé — cup by cup — to people standing on the roadside. Before that I was washing cars. Before that I was selling petrol. I did the jobs that most people do not talk about in their success stories because they are not glamorous enough.

But here is what nobody saw while all of that was happening.

I was reading.

It had started even before the streets. Growing up, my father had books in the house — and I read all of them. Every single one I could find. Something about those pages felt like a door into a world bigger than the one I was standing in.

So even when I was on the street hustling to survive, books were never far away. By 2015 and 2016 — while I was still a student, still figuring out how to make ends meet — I already had a private library. Not a shelf. A library.

For a long time the reading felt like seeds being planted in ground I could not see. I was absorbing ideas, frameworks, the thinking of people who had built things, led people, changed industries. But the harvest had not come yet.

Then I got into tech. I became a product designer — and everything shifted.

All those years of reading started speaking. The ideas I had absorbed about people, systems, how businesses work — all of it started coming out of me in rooms where it mattered. I worked with businesses locally and internationally, and I could see things other people in the room could not see. Not because I was smarter. Because I had been feeding my mind for years while others had not.

The reading had been working the whole time. I just had to get to the right environment for it to show up.

Today I run a business in the EdTech space. And I can trace a straight line from my father's bookshelf, to the streets, to every room I now walk into with confidence — and that line is made entirely of books.

But here is the honest part. Even as someone who loved reading since childhood, I struggled to finish books. I had to fight to build the discipline to finish. I had to create systems for myself just to stay on track.

That fight — that specific struggle — is exactly what HPRS is built to solve.

For the first time, I am opening my personal circle. Not to everyone. Only to the ones who are serious.

— Waribugo Godspower
Founder, HPRS  |  Product Designer  |  EdTech Entrepreneur

The Problem

This Is the Story of Someone You Know Very Well.

Her name is Chidi.

Chidi is smart. Everyone around her knows it. She is the one people call when they need advice. The one who always has an idea. The one who sees opportunities others walk past without blinking.

Two years ago Chidi decided she was going to be serious about her growth. She downloaded twelve books in one weekend. Atomic Habits. Rich Dad Poor Dad. Start With Why. Zero to One. All of them. She told herself this was the year.

She read the first chapter of Atomic Habits on a Sunday night and felt something shift inside her chest — that rare feeling of clarity. Of finally understanding something important.

She highlighted three sentences. Wrote a note in the margin. Fell asleep with the book on her chest.

By Wednesday she had not opened it again.

Not because she forgot. She remembered every day. She saw the app icon every time she unlocked her phone. But there was always something more urgent — a message to reply to, a task that could not wait, a day that ran longer than expected. The book sat there. Patient. Waiting.

Weeks passed. Then months. Chidi downloaded four more books in January — a new year, a new commitment, a new version of herself she was absolutely going to become this time. She joined two WhatsApp groups about reading. Both went quiet by February.

And through all of this — through the downloads and the highlights and the good intentions — nothing changed. Not her income. Not her confidence. Not her business. Not the way she made decisions or handled pressure or showed up in rooms that mattered.

Chidi is not lazy. She never was.

Chidi just had no system. No accountability. No environment designed for her growth. No one watching. No one expecting her to show up. She was trying to build discipline in a vacuum — and discipline does not survive a vacuum.

Here is the part that hurts most: Chidi knows the answers to most of her problems are sitting inside books she already downloaded. She just cannot get herself to finish them alone.
